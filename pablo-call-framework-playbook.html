The Core Idea

Pick one framework. Commit to it.

The thing that breaks calls isn't the framework — it's running two at once. A "goals first" open and a "biggest challenge first" open both work on their own. Blending them mid-call is what causes the prospect's answer to spill across future, current-state, and emotion all at once, with no rule for what to grab first.
The Structure

Current-reality-first, in order

1
Situation — Current Reality
Open with "What's the biggest challenge?" or "What's not working with your finances that you wish would?" — not a goals question. Stay here until it's fully mapped: what, how long, why it hasn't been fixed.
2
Problem — Awareness
Drill into what they just described. Ask "what do you mean by that?" on anything vague or loaded before going further. Don't assume you understand a phrase like "erratic spending" or "ignorant" — get it specific.
3
Solution — Awareness
Only once the problem is concrete: what have they tried, why didn't it work, what changed that made them reach out now.
4
Goal / Future Pace / Consequence
This is where future-state talk belongs. If the prospect brings it up earlier, acknowledge it and bookmark it — don't chase it out of order.
Principles

Why this works

You control the call — don't make yourself smaller to let them run it
The instinct to chase every interesting thread a prospect gives you feels like thoroughness. It's actually losing control. The fix isn't remembering everything they said — it's redirecting: acknowledge, then pull back to the current stage.
Clarity is for you, not a performance for them
Questions aren't a script to run — they're how you get clarity for yourself. If you can't explain their situation back clearly, you can't help them, no matter how much they've talked.
"If they just knew this is the problem and this is how it's going to be solved — take my money. That's clarity doing the work."
Loaded statements get a clarifying question, not a deep dive
When a prospect drops something identity-level ("I guess I'm just ignorant about it"), the next move is simple: "What do you mean by that? How so?" Going straight into what it means about who they are is getting ahead of the process — that comes later, once the situation is fully mapped.
Know where the lead came from before the call
Paid ad vs. organic, retargeting vs. direct-book, lead magnet vs. straight ad — each implies a different level of built-in buying intent. It changes how you open, not just what you say.
Quick Reference

Lines that work

Opening the current-reality question
"What's the biggest challenge for you right now when it comes to your finances? What's not working?"
Redirecting a prospect who's rambling across future/problem/emotion
"You gave me a lot there, and I appreciate the context — but I want to make sure I understand where you're at right now. What would you say is the one biggest challenge?"
Don't apologize for asking it again. Frame it as wanting to be clear for both of you.
On a loaded or identity-level statement
"What do you mean by that? How so?"
Use this before going deeper into what a statement like "erratic" or "ignorant" means about them as a person.
Two decision-makers, one not bought in ("the fourth wall")
"[Name], I see you're there. [Partner], I see you're not — what's missing for you? What would you need to know for this to be a yes for you too?"
Names the gap directly instead of pretending both are equally in. Turns it into the rep and the bought-in partner bringing the other one along, rather than arguing against both.
At a Glance

Do this / not that

Do
  • Pick current-reality-first or goals-first — one, not both
  • Ask "what do you mean by that?" before going deep
  • Redirect rambling answers back to the current stage
  • Bookmark future-state info for later, don't chase it early
  • Check lead source before the call to calibrate approach
Don't
  • Chase an identity-level statement before the situation is mapped
  • Let a prospect's tangent dictate what you ask next
  • Assume you understand a vague phrase without asking
  • Mix future-pace questions into the current-reality stage
  • Treat "I don't want to miss anything" as a reason to lose the frame
