Data and KPIs

How to define the right metrics, track them consistently, and use the data to make decisions — not just fill dashboards.

📐
Why Data Matters

You cannot improve what you cannot measure. Data tells you whether you have a top-of-funnel problem, a qualification problem, a call problem, or a follow-up problem. Without it, you are guessing at the wrong fix.

Every KPI on this list exists because it points to a specific action. If a number does not tell you what to do next, it is not a useful KPI.

The goal is not a perfect dashboard — it is faster diagnosis and better decisions. Track less, act on it more.

How to Define a KPI
A Good KPI Has Three Things

A clear definition. Everyone on the team should be able to describe exactly how it is measured — no interpretation required.

A specific target. Not "improve response rate" — a number. "10% response rate or above." Vague targets create vague accountability.

A linked action. If it goes below target, there is a specific thing you check or change. A metric without a response plan is just a number.

Diagnosing with Data

Low response rate: The messaging is not landing. Compare your best-performing first touch to your worst. What is the difference?

Low show rate: The pre-call sequence is not creating enough urgency, or the confirmation process is broken.

Low close rate: Pull the recordings. Find the exact moment the deal loses momentum — discovery, pitch, price, or follow-up.

Low deal size: Either reps are taking calls that are not a fit for the price point, or they are underselling the scope of what is possible.

What Not to Do with Data

Do not track a metric you have no plan to act on. A dashboard with 20 numbers and no actions is noise.

Do not use data to shame — use it to diagnose. The question is always "what do we fix," not "whose fault is this."

Do not wait until the end of the month to look at the numbers. Weekly reviews catch problems before they become expensive.
