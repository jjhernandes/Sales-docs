This is a simple, practical framework for discovery and closing calls. Use it to stay structured without sounding scripted.

Core principles

Diagnose before you prescribe

Most reps rush into pitching. Strong reps earn the right to present by understanding the buyer’s current state, desired state, blockers, and urgency first.

Control without overpowering

Good frame control feels calm, not pushy. Lead the call with confidence, but make the prospect feel understood and involved.

Questions create leverage

The best discovery questions do not just gather facts. They make the prospect think, feel the gap, and verbalize why change matters now.

Clarity closes deals

Confused prospects stall. Your job is to make the path, the problem, and the next step feel obvious.

1. Open strong

Goal: lower friction fast

Your first minute sets the emotional tone. Sound composed. Do not ramble. Let them feel they are in capable hands.

What to do

  • Thank them for the time.
  • Set a relaxed, confident tone.
  • Confirm the time boundary.
  • Transition quickly into purpose.
“Glad we could connect. We’ve got about 30 minutes. My goal is to understand what’s going on, see if there’s a real fit, and if there is, walk you through what I’d recommend. Sound fair?”

Common mistake

Over-explaining yourself at the start. The opening is not where you prove expertise. It is where you create safety and control.

2. Set the frame

Goal: establish process

Frame control is simply telling the prospect how the conversation will work so the call does not drift into randomness.

What to say

  • Explain the flow of the call.
  • Tell them you will ask direct questions.
  • Give yourself permission to challenge vague answers.
“I’m going to ask a few direct questions so I can understand the real situation, not just the surface-level version. Then if it makes sense, I’ll show you what I’d do.”

Why it matters

When you set the frame, you become the guide. Without a frame, the prospect unconsciously takes control and the call becomes reactive.

3. Diagnose deeply

Goal: uncover truth

This is the heart of the call. Your discovery should uncover current reality, desired outcome, emotional drivers, failed attempts, and decision dynamics.

Core discovery lanes

  • Current state: “Walk me through what’s happening today.”
  • Desired state: “What are you actually trying to accomplish?”
  • Gap: “What’s making that hard right now?”
  • Impact: “What is this costing you?”
  • Past action: “What have you already tried?”
  • Urgency: “Why solve this now instead of later?”

Coaching note

Do not machine-gun questions. Ask, listen, mirror their last few words, and pause. Prospects often reveal the real issue right after the pause.

“Say more about that.”
Rule: If the prospect has not clearly verbalized the problem in their own words, you are not ready to pitch.

4. Tie pain to cost

Goal: create urgency

Buyers move when the pain feels expensive enough. Help them connect the issue to money, time, energy, missed growth, stress, or identity.

Questions that deepen stakes

  • “How long has this been going on?”
  • “What happens if this stays the same for another 6 months?”
  • “What’s the downstream effect on the business or your team?”
  • “How frustrating is this on a day-to-day basis?”

Common mistake

Accepting mild pain. “It’s been annoying” is not enough. You need consequence. Consequence creates motion.

5. Present simply

Goal: connect solution to problem

Your presentation should feel like a natural continuation of discovery, not a switch into a canned demo. Only show what matters to their problem.

Simple presentation structure

  • Restate the problem.
  • Restate the desired outcome.
  • Show your mechanism.
  • Tie features to outcomes, not features to features.
“Based on what you said, the real problem isn’t just lead volume. It’s that your reps are working bad opportunities and the process breaks after first touch. Here’s how we fix that.”

Coaching note

If you cannot connect a slide, feature, or explanation directly to something the prospect said, cut it.

6. Handle objections

Goal: resolve the real concern

Objections are often incomplete truths. Do not swat them away. Slow down, isolate, and understand what is actually underneath the resistance.

Objection flow

  • Acknowledge: “Totally fair.”
  • Clarify: “When you say price, do you mean budget, priority, or uncertainty about ROI?”
  • Isolate: “Other than that, does this solve what you came on the call for?”
  • Respond: address the actual issue.

Useful line

“Help me understand what feels risky here.”

That question often gets you to the truth faster than arguing logic.

7. Close cleanly

Goal: secure a real next step

The close should feel like the logical outcome of the call, not a surprise attack. If the call was run well, the close becomes a decision, not a push.

Closing language

  • “Based on everything we talked through, does this feel like the right next move?”
  • “Do you want to move forward and get this in motion?”
  • “What do you need to feel comfortable making a decision today?”

Common mistake

Ending with vague softness like let me know what you think. Strong reps always leave the call with a defined outcome: yes, no, or a specific next step.

Quick cheat sheet

Basic call flow:

  • Open and lower tension
  • Set the frame
  • Diagnose the problem deeply
  • Expand the consequences
  • Present the solution simply
  • Handle objections by clarifying first
  • Close with a direct next step

Simple reminder: Slow the call down. Ask better questions. Make the problem real. Then make the decision easy.
